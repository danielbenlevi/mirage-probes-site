When Visual Understanding Is Not Visual

Paper

Overview

Vision-language models are often evaluated by whether they answer image-based questions correctly. However, VLMs can produce confident, often correct answers to visual questions even when no image is provided, a behavior known as mirage. We study whether that same mirage behavior leaves a detectable signature inside the model even when images are provided.

We introduce Mirage Probes, a contrastive probing framework for diagnosing these behaviors at the representation level. Across open-source VLMs and multiple VQA benchmarks, we find that mirage-associated generations are linearly decodable from image-present activations, including residual stream, MLP, post-attention, and attention-head sites. Thus, we empirically demonstrate that mirage mechanisms can still activate regardless of the presence of visual input.

Our findings indicate that mirage behavior is not a single failure mode. Some mirages appear to come from textual biases, where the question alone gives the model enough distributional evidence to answer without using the image. Others appear to come from spurious images, where the model behaves as though unsupported visual content exists in its latent representations. This distinction matters because the two mechanisms imply different fixes: cleaning benchmark text distributions may reduce textual shortcuts, but it is unlikely to eliminate mirages that arise from the creation of spurious images.

Dataset

To study mirage behavior inside VLMs, we construct paired with-image conversation datasets from model responses with and without the image. For each question, we compare the model’s image-conditioned response to its image-absent response. If the model gives essentially the same answer in both settings, we label the image-conditioned response as mirage-like. If the image-absent response explicitly refuses or expresses uncertainty because the image is missing, we label the corresponding image-conditioned response as non-mirage-like.

General mirage labels can be confounded by superficial text patterns. To reduce this problem, we build contrastive pairs. For each base image-question example, we generate light paraphrases that preserve the original meaning while perturbing the wording. We then keep cases where near-equivalent question variants produce both mirage-like and non-mirage-like responses. This gives us pairs that share the same image and nearly the same question semantics, but differ in mirage behavior.

We evaluate this framework on VQA-RAD, MMMU-Pro, and MedXpertQA. These benchmarks vary in how much information the question text itself provides. That variation becomes important later: datasets whose questions present rich distributional information appear to induce different mirage mechanisms than datasets whose questions require more specific visual evidence.

Probing

We aim to determine whether or not mirage behavior is encoded in the model’s internal activations. After generating a response, we feed the full image-question-response conversation back through the VLM and extract activations from several sites: the residual stream, MLP outputs, post-attention outputs, and individual attention heads. We then train lightweight probes to predict whether a response was mirage-like or non-mirage-like.

We compare several probing strategies. Linear probes test whether mirage behavior is recoverable as a simple direction in representation space. Two-layer MLP probes test whether the signal requires a nonlinear transformation. Concatenated-layer probes test whether the signal is distributed across layers. Difference probes test whether the representational shift caused by adding the image separates mirage-like from non-mirage-like examples.

We also include text-only baselines to check whether the probes are merely exploiting surface features in the generated responses. In particular, we train a Naive Bayes classifier over response unigrams and bigrams. Finally, we introduce the Prior Harnessing Index, or PHI, which measures how much the model’s confidence in the correct answer increases from the question text alone. PHI helps distinguish cases where the model believes it can answer from textual priors from cases where it must rely on visual or visually evoked information.

Results

We find that mirage behavior is consistently recoverable from image-present latent activations. Linear probes achieve above-chance accuracy across residual stream, MLP, post-attention, and attention-head targets in both Ovis and Qwen. Two-layer MLP probes do not meaningfully outperform linear probes, suggesting that the relevant signal is not hidden behind a complex nonlinear transformation. Difference probes are especially strong, motivating their use as classifiers.

The contrastive construction is crucial. Text-only Naive Bayes classifiers perform substantially worse in the contrastive setting, while representation probes remain strong. This suggests that our contrastive probes are not simply detecting shallow lexical artifacts in the model’s responses.

Benchmark-level patterns point to two different mirage regimes. Some mirages appear to reflect textual biases, where the model can extract enough answer-relevant information from the question alone. Others appear to reflect spurious images, where the model behaves as though unsupported visual content exists in its internal representations. Crucially, this distinction is not fully captured by human judgments of whether a question requires an image: what matters is how much useful information the model itself can extract from the text distribution. Our PHI analysis supports this view, suggesting that mirage mechanisms depend jointly on the question’s apparent visual dependence and the model’s own ability to harness textual priors.

Together, these results suggest that mirage behavior should not be treated as a single output-level pathology. Some mirages can plausibly be mitigated by reducing textual shortcuts, but others appear to live in the model’s internal visual representations. Improving faithful grounding will therefore require representational diagnostics and interventions, not only cleaner benchmarks or better prompting.
