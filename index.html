When Visual Understanding Is Not Visual

Mirage Probes studies a failure mode in vision-language models where the model answers image-grounded questions confidently even when the image is missing. The paper argues that this is not one behavior, but two: textual bias and spurious image generation.

Abstract

What the paper shows

The paper argues that a VLM can appear visually grounded while actually relying on language priors or internally fabricated visual content. To test that hypothesis, the authors introduce Mirage Probes, a contrastive probing setup that compares paraphrased questions with matched mirage and non-mirage labels on the same image.

Across the probe suite, mirage behavior is recoverable from internal activations in the residual stream, MLP blocks, post-attention outputs, and attention heads. A naive Bayes text baseline is weaker, which supports the claim that the signal is not just a lexical artifact.

Practical implication

Text distribution cleaning can help with the textual-bias regime, but it will not address spurious images because that failure mode lives in the model's visual representations.

Representation-level intervention needed

Mechanisms

The paper decomposes mirage behavior into two regimes

Textual bias

The model answers from language priors, dataset regularities, or benchmark shortcuts without really engaging the image.

  • Best addressed with text-distribution cleaning
  • High confidence can still be unsupported
  • Often exposed by PHI and cross-benchmark shifts

Spurious images

The model constructs false visual content in latent space and behaves as though that content were real.

  • Needs representation-level intervention
  • Cannot be removed by text-only filtering
  • Shows up as image-reliant mirage behavior

Results

What stands out in the experiments

Contrastive probes stay above chance

The contrastive setup reduces surface-level confounds while still exposing a recoverable signal in internal activations.

Naive Bayes is weaker

A text-only baseline fails to recover the same pattern, which argues against a purely lexical explanation.

VQA-RAD behaves differently

Benchmark patterns suggest that VQA-RAD contains more image-reliant examples, which helps separate the spurious-image regime from text priors.

PHI adds an interpretable axis

Prior Harnessing Index estimates how much answer confidence comes from text alone, and it correlates with mirage behavior in the most image-dependent benchmark.
