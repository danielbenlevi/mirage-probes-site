When Visual Understanding Is Not Visual

Daniel Ben-Levi1y, Judah Goldfeder1, Weiliang Zhao1, Raz Lapid2, Amit Levi3, Allen G Roush4, Ravid Shwartz-Ziv5, Hod Lipson1

1 Columbia University    2 Intuit    3 Technion    4 Thoughtworks    5 New York University

Paper

Overview

Vision-language models can answer image-based questions confidently, and often correctly, even when no image is provided. The paper treats this mirage behavior as a benchmark inflation problem rather than a simple output error, and argues that it splits into two mechanisms: textual biases, where the model answers from language priors, and spurious images, where it constructs false visual content in latent space. Mirage Probes is introduced as a contrastive probing framework to expose that distinction inside the model rather than only at the output level.

Dataset

Section 3.2 builds the probe datasets from paired with-image and without-image responses. The paper compares the model's response with the image present and with the image removed, then assigns a binary label indicating whether the with-image response is mirage-like. The authors organize both contrastive and all-examples pools across VQA-RAD, MMMU-Pro, and MedXpertQA. The contrastive setting keeps paraphrased question variants with matched labels on the same image, while the broader all-examples setting is filtered and split to support probe training and baseline evaluation.

Probing

Sections 3.3 and 3.4 probe the model's internal activations with lightweight classifiers. The authors test logistic regression probes on the residual stream, MLP activations, concatenated layer activations, and activation differences, along with probes over attention head and post-attention outputs. They compare these linear probes to two-layer MLP probes and to a text-only Naive Bayes baseline. In addition, they define the Prior Harnessing Index, or PHI, as a text-only measure of how much the model can recover the correct answer from the question distribution alone.

Results

The main experimental result is that mirage behavior is recoverable from internal activations, and the contrastive setup reduces surface confounds enough to make that signal meaningful. In Table 1, difference probes are the strongest across both Ovis and Qwen, while residual and attention probes remain clearly above chance in the contrastive setting. The all-examples setting is often higher, but it is also more likely to reflect shallow cues rather than the mechanism of interest.

Table 2 shows why the authors emphasize the contrastive construction. The Naive Bayes baseline is consistently weaker than the probing models, which supports the claim that the probes are not just reading off unigram or bigram response features. The paper uses this gap to argue that mirage behavior is encoded in the representation space rather than being reducible to surface lexical patterns.

Table 3 separates the datasets by perceived image reliance. VQA-RAD contains the clearest image-reliant pattern, while MMMU-Pro and MedXpertQA show a much larger share of examples that can be answered from text alone. This benchmark-level difference is one reason the paper argues for two regimes: textual bias and spurious images do not behave the same way under probing.

Table 4 connects PHI to the mirage labels and compares reliance-conditioned training. VQA-RAD is the benchmark where low PHI and mirage behavior are most tightly connected, which matches the paper's claim that some mirages arise from latent visual assumptions rather than only from text priors. The reliance-conditioned experiment does not fully eliminate the gap, which reinforces the conclusion that text-only cleaning can help one regime but not the other.

Taken together, the results support the paper's conclusion that mirage behavior is not a single failure mode. Contrastive probes recover a linearly decodable signal, Naive Bayes remains weaker, and benchmark differences suggest that textual priors and spurious images are distinct mechanisms that require different interventions.
